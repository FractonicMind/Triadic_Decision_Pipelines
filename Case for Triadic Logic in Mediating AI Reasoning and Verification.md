# Beyond True or False: A Case for Triadic Logic in Mediating AI Reasoning and Verification

## The Foundational Dichotomy: Probabilistic Reasoning vs. Deterministic Verification

The landscape of artificial intelligence is defined by a fundamental tension between two distinct yet increasingly intertwined paradigms of reasoning: probabilistic cognition and deterministic verification. Modern AI systems, particularly those based on large-scale machine learning, operate on a foundation of statistical inference and sequence prediction, generating outputs based on learned likelihoods [[201](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2412.06512)]. This approach has proven exceptionally powerful for tasks involving pattern recognition, natural language understanding, and creative problem-solving, where ambiguity and variability are inherent [[122](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/davarn-morrison-14b93b263_this-new-nature-medicine-study-confirms-what-activity-7428088175970902016-Zey_), [221](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3533378)]. However, in precision-critical applications—from verifying software security to making life-altering medical diagnoses—this probabilistic nature creates a significant challenge. These domains demand a different standard of truth: absolute certainty, or exact symbolic correctness [[7](https://arxiv.org/html/2409.14274v1), [9](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.18050v1)]. This requirement is met through deterministic verification mechanisms, which rely on rigorous logical frameworks built upon binary logic (true/false). The friction between these two systems—the probabilistic generator and the binary verifier—is at the heart of the challenge in building truly reliable AI.

Probabilistic reasoning, as employed by contemporary AI, is fundamentally about navigating uncertainty. Large Language Models (LLMs), for instance, generate text by predicting the next most likely token in a sequence based on vast corpora of training data [[201](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2412.06512)]. This process is inherently non-deterministic; it optimizes for likelihood, not for provable truth [[201](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2412.06512)]. While this allows them to produce fluent and contextually relevant language, it also makes them susceptible to "hallucinations," where they generate information that is plausible-sounding but factually incorrect [[201](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2412.06512)]. Furthermore, neural networks often exhibit dangerous overconfidence when faced with out-of-distribution (OOD) data, assigning high probabilities to predictions that are demonstrably wrong [[33](https://dl.acm.org/doi/proceedings/10.1145/3746027?tocHeading=heading4)]. This probabilistic nature means that an LLM cannot provide a definitive guarantee about the correctness of its output. It can only offer a score indicating its confidence level, which may not always correlate with actual accuracy [[68](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/dhruv-suyamprakasam-49005b17_measuring-confidence-calibration-in-medical-activity-7426646334478372864-mTlc), [162](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.24202v1)]. This limitation is critical in contexts where a failure is not a mere error in performance but a catastrophic event. For example, in medicine, AI failures are often due to contextual errors, where the model lacks the nuanced clinical reasoning required for a specific patient scenario, leading to unreliable outputs despite high confidence scores [[121](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/pmwcintl_medical-ai-doesnt-fail-in-the-clinic-because-activity-7424889159850483712-5c8j), [122](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/davarn-morrison-14b93b263_this-new-nature-medicine-study-confirms-what-activity-7428088175970902016-Zey_)].

In stark contrast, deterministic verification provides a framework for achieving mathematical and logical certainty. This paradigm is the bedrock of fields like formal mathematics, software engineering, and safety-critical systems design. Interactive Theorem Provers (ITPs) such as Isabelle, Coq, Lean, and HOL Light exemplify this approach [[1](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.17017v1), [6](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.17017)]. In these systems, theorems are expressed in a formal language, and proofs are constructed step-by-step according to a set of rigorous, machine-checkable logical rules [[138](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.09939v1), [141](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.09939)]. Every proof attempt is subjected to a binary verdict: it is either accepted as valid or rejected as invalid [[107](https://www.mdpi.com/2504-2289/10/1/38)]. This all-or-nothing nature ensures that if a proof is accepted, its conclusion is guaranteed to be correct, assuming the underlying logical system is sound. Similarly, in the domain of autonomous vehicles, safety decisions are often framed in terms of success or failure, with risk tolerance being a crucial parameter in the computational model [[19](https://www.mdpi.com/2624-8921/6/1/7)]. The goal of safety assurance for automated vehicles involves joint consideration of the application of automated driving and the technology of AI, focusing on safety assurance [[75](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.18328v1)]. Even in medical diagnostics, while AI models provide probabilistic assessments, the final diagnostic category often carries significant weight, and communicating uncertainty is a critical challenge [[10](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7785732/), [39](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9352100/)]. Regulatory bodies like the FDA distinguish between analytical performance (the device's ability to perform as intended under controlled conditions) and clinical effectiveness (its benefit to patients in the real world), highlighting the gap between statistical prediction and verified utility [[44](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/344202528_The_state_of_artificial_intelligence-based_FDA-approved_medical_devices_and_algorithms_an_online_database), [133](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0972629226000100)].

This dichotomy creates a systemic friction point in hybrid AI systems. The probabilistic generator produces a stream of potential solutions, hypotheses, or actions, each accompanied by a degree of uncertainty. This stream is then funneled into a deterministic verifier, which acts as a strict gatekeeper, applying binary checks for syntactic correctness, logical consistency, or adherence to safety constraints. If the verifier rejects the output, the system must discard it and typically try again, often without any insight into why the specific path was invalid. If it accepts the output, the system has no guarantee that the underlying probabilistic model was correct, only that it happened to conform to the verifier's rigid rules. This interaction forces a premature and often inappropriate decision on an inherently uncertain output. The probabilistic result is flattened into a simple pass/fail verdict, losing the valuable nuance of its original confidence score and the context of its generation. This process can be inefficient, as it may repeatedly reject plausible but imperfectly formed candidates, and brittle, as it offers no graceful way to handle situations that fall into a gray area between clear validity and clear invalidity. The core challenge lies in bridging this gap between the continuous, probabilistic nature of AI cognition and the discrete, binary nature of formal verification, a task for which a third logical state may provide a more elegant and effective solution.

## Current Hybrid Architectures and Their Binary Constraints

Modern AI systems attempting to bridge the gap between probabilistic reasoning and deterministic verification have adopted several hybrid architectural patterns. These designs generally treat the deterministic verifier as a filter or validator applied to the outputs generated by a probabilistic model. While functional, these approaches are fundamentally constrained by their reliance on binary decision pipelines, which force a premature acceptance or rejection of uncertain outputs. An analysis of these current architectures reveals both their capabilities and their inherent limitations, providing a strong motivation for exploring alternative coordination mechanisms.

One common approach is **constrained decoding**, where the generative process of a model like an LLM is guided during inference to adhere to specific structural or semantic constraints [[168](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/19d102f2cdace2160752d3e25ade620859406e7f)]. For example, a model might be tasked with generating a piece of code; instead of just predicting the next token, the system can use a verifier to check if the partial code generated so far is syntactically valid after each step. If the syntax is broken, the model is discouraged from continuing down that path, often by penalizing the corresponding tokens. This method effectively integrates a form of lightweight, online verification. However, it operates on a binary principle: the code is either syntactically correct or it is not. It struggles with more complex properties, such as semantic correctness or adherence to a high-level specification, which require deeper, more resource-intensive verification. The constraint itself is typically a hard rule, offering no mechanism to handle cases where the structure is ambiguous or requires further clarification.

Another prevalent pattern involves **programmatic validation** and structured output formats. Here, the probabilistic model is prompted to generate an output in a predefined, structured format like JSON or XML. A subsequent programmatic step parses this output and validates its structure and the types of its fields. This is widely used in agentic systems where an LLM agent needs to interact with APIs or databases [[62](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.09774v1)]. If the generated JSON is malformed or contains a string where a number is expected, the entire output is rejected. This is a robust method for ensuring interoperability but again relies on a binary pass/fail criterion. It cannot gracefully handle a situation where a field is present but its value is highly uncertain or requires expert judgment. The system is forced to either accept a potentially flawed input or fail entirely, discarding the user's intent along with the malformed data.

A more sophisticated class of hybrid systems employs **formal verification pipelines**, particularly in the context of automated theorem proving and code synthesis [[109](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/389821642_Local_Look-Ahead_Guidance_via_Verifier-in-the-Loop_for_Automated_Theorem_Proving), [137](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.06512v1)]. In these setups, an LLM might act as a "copilot," proposing proof steps or code snippets for a human expert or a formal tool to verify [[285](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.12534v3), [311](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.04653v1)]. The formal verifier, such as an ITP, then provides a definitive answer: the proposed step is either accepted or rejected [[107](https://www.mdpi.com/2504-2289/10/1/38)]. Recent work has explored verifier-in-the-loop reinforcement learning, where the proof assistant serves as a binary reward signal for an LLM-based prover [[107](https://www.mdpi.com/2504-2289/10/1/38)]. While this creates a powerful feedback loop for improving the model's reasoning, the interaction remains strictly binary. There is no state for a proposal that is neither clearly correct nor clearly incorrect—a proof step that is plausible but relies on a non-obvious lemma, for instance. Such a step would likely be rejected, forcing the model to start over without the opportunity to seek clarification. Benchmarks like VeriBench highlight the difficulty of this end-to-end verification task, underscoring the challenges in creating seamless integration between probabilistic generators and deterministic verifiers [[27](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=rWkGFmnSNl)].

Even in precision-critical domains like **autonomous systems**, a similar dynamic is observed. Autonomous vehicles (AVs) rely on probabilistic perception systems to predict the trajectories of other objects [[53](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1109/ICRA.2019.8794130)]. These probabilistic predictions must feed into a decision-making module that executes deterministic actions like braking or steering [[97](https://www.cell.com/iscience/fulltext/S2589-0042(22)00975-0)]. Safety protocols often involve checking if the predicted risk of collision exceeds a predefined threshold; if it does, a braking action is triggered. This is a binary safety check. However, AV perception systems are known to have significant error rates, sometimes up to 30%, and perception failures are a leading cause of crashes [[18](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0924271622003367), [163](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1002/rob.70108)]. The field of Safety of the Intended Functionality (SOTIF) specifically addresses risks arising from functional insufficiencies and incorrect assumptions, which inherently involve states of uncertainty that a simple binary threshold cannot adequately capture [[144](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2095809924000274), [284](https://www.engineering.org.cn/engi/EN/10.1016/j.eng.2023.10.011)]. Systematic reviews identify grand challenges in the verification of autonomous systems precisely because their behavior emerges from complex interactions between probabilistic components and deterministic controllers [[65](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.14155), [67](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/386021009_Grand_Challenges_in_the_Verification_of_Autonomous_Systems)].

These examples reveal a consistent pattern: current hybrid architectures use deterministic verification as a blunt instrument to prune the space of possibilities generated by a probabilistic model. They succeed in filtering out grossly incorrect or syntactically invalid outputs, but they fail to manage the spectrum of uncertainty that lies in between. When a probabilistic output is ambiguous, incomplete, or falls into a gray area, the binary pipeline is forced to make a suboptimal choice. It may prematurely reject a useful but imperfect output, leading to wasted computation and a poor user experience. Alternatively, it may accept an output that technically passes a surface-level check but fails a deeper, unstated requirement, leading to a silent failure. The absence of a formal mechanism to represent and manage a "pending" or "deferred" state means that these systems lack a crucial capability for graceful degradation and intelligent error handling. They are designed to either succeed completely or fail entirely, leaving no room for the nuanced, intermediate judgments that are often necessary in complex, real-world environments. This fundamental constraint highlights the need for a more expressive coordination layer that can better mediate between the probabilistic and deterministic worlds.

## Historical and Theoretical Foundations of Triadic Logic

The notion of a logical system extending beyond the classical binary values of true and false is not a recent invention but has deep historical roots in 20th-century logic. The development of many-valued logics, pioneered by figures such as Charles Sanders Peirce, Jan Łukasiewicz, and Stephen Kleene, provides a robust theoretical foundation for considering a third logical state as a viable mechanism for managing uncertainty. Their work was driven by philosophical inquiries into the nature of possibility, modality, and computability, which directly address the challenges posed by probabilistic AI.

Charles Sanders Peirce (1839–1914), a foundational figure in American pragmatism and logic, was one of the earliest thinkers to explore logical systems beyond the dyadic. His work moved away from the purely Boolean calculus towards more complex structures that could capture the nuances of reasoning [[85](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/peirce-logic/)]. Peirce's investigations into triadic relations and his development of Existential Graphs, a visual notation for logic, incorporated modal concepts like "possible falsity" through the use of a "broken cut" [[256](https://www.jstor.org/stable/27324880)]. His broader philosophical project aimed to develop a logic capable of describing a universe that is in a constant state of becoming, evolving, and containing elements of chance and continuity [[86](https://www.jstor.org/stable/10.2979/trancharpeirsoc.57.2.01)]. While his three-valued experiments were part of a larger effort to create a logic for modality and continuity, his general move away from purely binary and even ternary relations provided a profound philosophical precedent for thinking in more complex logical structures [[156](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/331905803_An_Application_of_Peircean_Triadic_Logic_Modelling_Vagueness), [157](https://www.academia.edu/17251772/Introduction_to_Peirces_Logic_and_Semeiotics)]. Peirce's work suggests that a simple true/false dichotomy is insufficient for capturing the full richness of reality and reasoning, laying an essential groundwork for later formalizations of multi-valued systems [[158](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/peirce/)].

Jan Łukasiewicz (1878–1956), a Polish logician and a founder of the Warsaw School of Logic, is widely recognized as a principal founder of many-valued logic [[153](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/lukasiewicz/)]. In a short note published in 1920, he introduced the first explicit system of three-valued logic [[57](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-manyvalued/)]. His primary motivation was philosophical: to resolve paradoxes related to future contingents. For instance, consider the statement "There will be a sea battle tomorrow." According to the principle of bivalence, this statement must be either true now or false now. But if it is necessarily true or false, then the future seems predetermined, contradicting our sense of free will. Łukasiewicz proposed a third truth value, interpreted as "possible" or "indeterminate," to break this dilemma. A statement about the future could be neither definitively true nor false but could have the potential to become one or the other [[154](https://www.jstor.org/stable/20013107)]. This innovation provided a formal mathematical framework for reasoning about indeterminacy and possibility, demonstrating that logical systems could be developed that did not rely on the law of bivalence. His work opened up a new branch of non-classical logic and inspired decades of research into the semantics and axiomatics of multi-valued systems [[167](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1057/9781137030894_2?fromPaywallRec=true)].

Stephen Cole Kleene (1909–1994) developed a strong three-valued logic (often denoted K3) primarily for practical, computational reasons within the field of mathematical logic and computability theory [[60](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225797422_Three-valued_logic_for_software_specification_and_validation), [224](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/266428078_Partial_Logic)]. His work was concerned with modeling the behavior of partial functions, which are functions that may not be defined for all possible inputs—for example, a division function that is undefined when the denominator is zero, or a recursive function that may not terminate for certain inputs [[59](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/spr2021/entries/recursive-functions/), [60](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225797422_Three-valued_logic_for_software_specification_and_validation)]. In Kleene's logic, the third truth value represents "undefined" or "unknown" [[222](http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0890540106000617/pdf?md5=594d12fda08a07fd473b6339ece50685&pid=1-s2.0-S0890540106000617-main.pdf)]. This value propagates through logical operations in a specific way, known as the "strong Kleene tables," which ensures that if any component of a compound expression is undefined, the entire expression is considered undefined [[151](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1909.08232), [224](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/266428078_Partial_Logic)]. This property is extremely useful for reasoning about programs and algorithms, as it allows for a formal treatment of computations that might fail to produce a result. Kleene's logic thus provides a direct and powerful bridge to computer science, offering a principled way to reason about the "undefinedness" that arises naturally in AI models and software systems. The existence of complete proof systems for extensions of Kleene's logic further indicates that a triadic reasoning layer could be made formally sound and amenable to automated reasoning techniques [[61](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2112.04436), [152](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/978-3-7091-9461-4.pdf)].

The collective work of these logicians demonstrates that the desire to move beyond binary logic stems from a genuine need to model and reason about situations that are not simply true or false. For the purpose of integrating AI, Kleene's logic appears particularly relevant due to its direct connection to computation and partial functions. It offers a formal language for expressing the "failure to compute" or "inapplicable" states that are common in probabilistic models. Meanwhile, the philosophical insights from Peirce and Łukasiewicz provide a conceptual justification for acknowledging a state of genuine uncertainty or indeterminacy. Together, they provide a comprehensive theoretical toolkit for designing a triadic coordination layer that can serve as a more nuanced interface between probabilistic generators and deterministic verifiers.

## Architectural Integration of a Triadic Decision Layer

Integrating a triadic logical decision layer into an AI system requires identifying specific points in the processing pipeline where a probabilistic output must be reconciled with a deterministic requirement. The introduction of a third state, tentatively labeled "verification pending," offers a way to represent an intermediate judgment that is neither fully accepted nor outright rejected. This allows the system to defer a final decision, triggering a more sophisticated response than a simple retry or discard. Several concrete architectural locations within an AI system are suitable for hosting such a layer, each offering unique opportunities to improve efficiency, reliability, and transparency.

One of the most promising locations for a triadic layer is within the **decoder control mechanism** of a generative model like an LLM. During autoregressive generation, the decoder produces a probability distribution over the vocabulary for the next token. Standard decoding strategies, such as greedy search (always picking the highest-probability token) or top-k sampling, operate on a purely probabilistic basis. A triadic-aware decoder could modify this process by incorporating a verifier or a set of constraints into the selection process. For example, if a candidate token is highly probable but violates a syntactic rule checked by a lightweight verifier, instead of being assigned a probability of zero (and thus being ignored), it could be flagged with a "verification pending" status. This would signal the generation process to either explore lower-probability alternatives that satisfy the constraint, or to pause and invoke a more expensive, specialized reasoning module to resolve the ambiguity. This approach prevents the premature pruning of potentially valuable generation paths and allows the model to navigate the trade-off between statistical likelihood and logical consistency more gracefully.

Another key architectural site is the **execution gate**, a module positioned between the AI's reasoning engine and its actuators or output interfaces. Before a high-impact action is executed—for example, deploying a piece of code, administering a medical treatment suggestion, or commanding a robotic arm—the action would pass through this gate. An execution gate operating on a triadic logic system would have three possible outputs:
*   **Accepted:** The action has been fully verified against all safety and logical constraints and is deemed safe to execute.
*   **Rejected:** The action violates a critical, non-negotiable constraint and is forbidden.
*   **Verification Pending:** The action's outcome is uncertain or its full implications cannot be determined by the available information. In this state, the system would not simply fail but would trigger a pre-defined policy. This could involve pausing execution, escalating the request to a human-in-the-loop supervisor for approval, invoking a more thorough but computationally expensive verification procedure, or executing a safer default action while awaiting confirmation [[180](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.27190v1)]. This mechanism is analogous to safety-critical software practices where high-impact actions are gated and monitored [[180](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.27190v1)].

Within more complex reasoning systems, such as those employing chain-of-thought or agentic architectures, a triadic layer can be integrated into the **reasoning graph** itself. Each node in the graph represents an intermediate step of reasoning. Instead of assuming every step is definitively true or false, nodes can be annotated with a truth state. A reasoning step that appears plausible based on the model's probabilistic associations but lacks sufficient supporting evidence from its sources could be marked "verification pending". This would prevent the propagation of errors through the entire chain of reasoning. Upon encountering a "pending" node, the agent could be programmed to backtrack, query a different knowledge source, or ask clarifying questions before proceeding. This transforms the reasoning process from a blind, forward-propagating chain into a more interactive and self-correcting graph, where uncertainty at any point can trigger a local investigation rather than a global failure. This is particularly relevant for multi-step agent systems where uncertainty can propagate and amplify across sequential decisions [[196](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josepheoliver_prompt-verification-compliance-and-trust-activity-7420538137183133696-lOaf), [291](https://aclanthology.org/2025.acl-long.302.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com)].

Finally, triadic logic could enhance **hybrid neuro-symbolic systems** that explicitly combine neural network-based pattern matching with symbolic reasoning engines. In such frameworks, the neural component often generates queries or hypotheses for the symbolic engine to evaluate. A prominent example is QRS, which uses autonomous agents to generate CodeQL queries for analyzing software security [[62](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.09774v1)]. In this context, a generated query could be evaluated not just for syntactic correctness but for semantic clarity. A query that is syntactically valid but semantically ambiguous could be routed to a human expert for clarification via a "verification pending" state, rather than being discarded as invalid. This creates a more collaborative human-AI workflow, leveraging the strengths of both. Similarly, in LLM-powered theorem provers interacting with ITPs like Isabelle, a proposed proof step that is syntactically acceptable but logically obscure could be flagged for human review, preserving potentially insightful but poorly articulated ideas that would otherwise be lost [[285](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.12534v3), [311](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.04653v1)].

While these architectural integrations offer significant potential, they also introduce practical challenges, primarily concerning computational overhead. Adding a third state and the associated logic for managing transitions between states will inevitably increase the complexity of the system. One study noted that integrating a symbolic reasoning engine into a neural network approach could add approximately 35% to the computational cost [[36](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-24350-8)]. A triadic layer would likely incur similar or greater overhead, necessitating careful design to balance the benefits of increased reliability against the costs of latency and resource consumption [[31](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Study-of-overhead-on-ML-benchmarks_fig4_221632994), [189](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2505.19797v1)]. Nonetheless, the potential for creating more robust, transparent, and efficient AI systems in precision-critical domains makes the investigation of these architectural patterns a compelling avenue for future research.

## Comparative Analysis with Probabilistic Uncertainty Methods

The proposition of a triadic logical layer for coordinating AI must be rigorously evaluated against the existing, dominant methods for handling uncertainty. Modern AI systems, particularly in fields like medicine and autonomous driving, heavily rely on probabilistic approaches such as Bayesian probability, confidence calibration, and abstention mechanisms [[32](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13369-025-10887-3), [89](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.15850)]. These methods aim to quantify the model's uncertainty and provide a measure of confidence in its outputs. A comparative analysis reveals that while these probabilistic techniques are powerful, they differ significantly from triadic logic in their approach, offering distinct advantages and disadvantages.

Probabilistic methods, especially confidence calibration, operate on a continuous scale. They assign a numerical probability (e.g., 0.85) to a prediction, representing the model's belief in its own correctness [[68](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/dhruv-suyamprakasam-49005b17_measuring-confidence-calibration-in-medical-activity-7426646334478372864-mTlc)]. This score is ideally calibrated, meaning that if the model makes a hundred predictions with a confidence of 85%, approximately 85 of them should be correct [[247](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12897117/)]. Techniques like evidential deep learning and conformal prediction are being developed to provide more principled ways to estimate this uncertainty [[235](https://openreview.net/forum?id=YcML3rJl0N), [240](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/13/20/3351)]. The primary advantage of this approach is its granularity. A confidence score provides a rich, quantitative measure of uncertainty that can be used for sophisticated risk management and decision-making. For example, in medical AI, actionable confidence rests on three elements: calibration, uncertainty signaling, and deferral [[70](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050577125000441)]. A low-confidence case can be flagged for human review, which is a form of managed uncertainty. However, a key limitation is the difficulty of interpretation and communication. A score of 0.85 is mathematically precise but can be ambiguous in practice. It still implies a degree of acceptance and does not easily translate into a clear operational policy like "defer this decision." Moreover, these methods are fundamentally descriptive—they describe the model's uncertainty—but do not prescribe a structured, categorical response to it. The boundary between "high" and "low" confidence is often arbitrary, and the transition is smooth, lacking the crisp distinction that can be useful for defining clear system behaviors.

Abstention mechanisms are a more direct application of this idea. They define a hard threshold; if the model's confidence falls below this threshold, it abstains from making a prediction altogether [[14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12911-021-01655-y), [159](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.15391v1)]. This is functionally very close to the "rejected" state in a triadic system. Its strength is its simplicity and its ability to avoid making risky, low-confidence predictions. However, it is a blunt instrument. Abstention forces a binary choice: predict or don't predict. It discards potentially valuable information contained in a low-confidence but not-zero-confidence output. There is no middle ground to say, "This is uncertain, but here is some partial information that might be useful while we gather more evidence." This makes it less flexible than a triadic model, which could allow for a more graded response to uncertainty.

Triadic logic offers a fundamentally different paradigm: **explicit state management**. Instead of relying on a continuous measure of confidence, it introduces a discrete, named state—"verification pending"—to represent a specific condition: a deferred judgment due to incomplete evidence or pending validation. This provides superior **expressiveness and policy control**. A system designer can write unambiguous rules: "If the state is 'Accepted', execute the action. If the state is 'Rejected', log the error and halt. If the state is 'Pending', route the request to the human-in-the-loop module for escalation." This creates a much clearer and more structured way to handle uncertainty than a probabilistic score alone. The "pending" state is not just a low-confidence score; it is a semantic label that signifies a specific type of problem and triggers a specific, well-defined recovery path. This can lead to more transparent and trustworthy systems, as the reason for a delay or refusal is explicitly stated.

The following table summarizes the key differences between these approaches:

| Feature | Probabilistic Confidence Calibration | Abstention Mechanisms | Triadic Logic ("Pending") |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Core Representation** | Continuous numerical score (e.g., 0.0 to 1.0) [[68](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/dhruv-suyamprakasam-49005b17_measuring-confidence-calibration-in-medical-activity-7426646334478372864-mTlc)] | Binary decision (Predict/Do Not Predict) [[159](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.15391v1)] | Discrete, named state ('Accepted', 'Rejected', 'Pending') |
| **Handling Ambiguity** | Describes uncertainty quantitatively; low scores signal issues [[89](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.15850)] | Discards ambiguous/potentially incorrect outputs [[14](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12911-021-01655-y)] | Explicitly defers judgment for further investigation [[180](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.27190v1)] |
| **Policy Definition** | Requires setting arbitrary thresholds to trigger actions (e.g., human review) [[70](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050577125000441)] | Simple: predict above threshold, abstain below. Lacks nuance. | Highly explicit and structured policies based on named states [[180](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.27190v1)] |
| **Information Preservation** | Preserves the degree of uncertainty in the confidence score. | Discards the output and its associated (low) confidence score. | Can preserve the probabilistic output while flagging it for review. |
| **Interpretability** | Can be difficult to interpret and communicate to non-experts [[162](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.24202v1)]. | High: the decision is binary and simple to understand. | Potentially high: "pending" is a clear semantic label for a deferred state. |

Despite these advantages, triadic logic faces significant counterarguments. The strongest argument against it is that existing probabilistic methods are sufficient. Researchers argue that advances in uncertainty quantification, such as evidential deep learning, can already provide robust estimates of uncertainty that can guide system behavior, potentially obviating the need for a new logical framework [[235](https://openreview.net/forum?id=YcML3rJl0N), [283](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2409.04720)]. Another major hurdle is the **lack of empirical evidence**. While the theoretical foundations are solid, there are few, if any, large-scale studies demonstrating the practical benefits of triadic architectures in production AI systems. Most of the evidence for the utility of a "pending" state comes from observing its use in other domains, not from its direct application in AI [[110](https://tsaco.bmj.com/content/11/1/e002272), [279](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8170738/)]. Finally, the **computational complexity** of implementing and verifying a triadic system is a serious concern. Adding another state and the logic to manage it increases system complexity and may introduce unacceptable overhead, especially in latency-sensitive applications [[31](https://www.researchgate.net/figure/Study-of-overhead-on-ML-benchmarks_fig4_221632994), [189](https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/2505.19797v1)].

In essence, the debate is between orthogonality and integration. Probabilistic methods add a dimension of confidence to the existing binary output, keeping the core logic intact. Triadic logic proposes a change in the fundamental structure of the decision space itself. The choice depends on whether the problem is best solved by refining a continuous measure or by introducing a discrete, meaningful category of deferment. For safety-critical systems where clear, auditable policies for handling uncertainty are paramount, the discrete, semantically rich nature of a triadic state may offer advantages that continuous probabilities alone cannot provide.

## Practical Manifestations and Future Research Directions

The theoretical appeal of a triadic coordination layer gains substantial weight from its practical manifestation in various precision-critical domains. The widespread, albeit often informal, use of a "pending verification" state in fields like medicine, autonomous systems, and software engineering provides strong empirical support for its utility. Analyzing these real-world applications reveals a recurring need for a mechanism to gracefully handle ambiguity and incomplete information, reinforcing the hypothesis that such a state is not merely a theoretical curiosity but a pragmatic necessity. Building on this evidence, several concrete future research directions emerge to move the concept from theory toward practical implementation.

The domain of **medicine** provides perhaps the most compelling evidence. Medical practice is replete with situations requiring a deferred judgment. Clinical documentation systems explicitly allow for notes such as "pending verification" to account for results that are not yet available or confirmed [[110](https://tsaco.bmj.com/content/11/1/e002272), [112](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/352062888_Development_and_implementation_of_an_LIS-based_validation_system_for_autoverification_toward_zero_defects_in_the_automated_reporting_of_laboratory_test_results)]. In the context of AI-assisted diagnosis, confidence calibration is critical, and a truly actionable confidence metric includes a mechanism for deferral [[70](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S3050577125000441)]. Studies on medical AI show that models can agree with FDA-cleared devices on most cases but still fail to detect certain conditions, necessitating a human-in-the-loop review [[73](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-025-02007-0)]. Frameworks like BODHI advocate for ethical abstention mechanisms in medical AI to prevent harm from erroneous predictions [[72](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/smithheavner_medicalai-regulatoryscience-samd-activity-7415410516195463170-odD4)]. The regulatory processes of the FDA are also built around this concept. Post-approval studies (PAS) are mandated to verify the long-term safety and effectiveness of medical devices [[132](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfpma/pma_pas.cfm?t_id=415)], and drug approvals often involve requests for pending verification of clinical benefits [[296](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2405.12437)]. Even in post-market surveillance, detected safety signals trigger investigations rather than immediate conclusions, embodying a state of provisional concern [[211](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/heatherkooiker_medicaldevices-patientsafety-clinicalevidence-activity-7425542236039430144-FjRZ)]. This pervasive use of "pending" in medicine underscores the high cost of premature binary decisions in life-and-death contexts.

Similarly, the development and deployment of **autonomous systems** highlight the critical need for managing uncertainty. Perception systems in autonomous vehicles (AVs) are known to have error rates of up to 30% under certain conditions, and perception failures are a primary contributor to crashes [[18](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0924271622003367), [163](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1002/rob.70108)]. The field of Safety of the Intended Functionality (SOTIF) was created specifically to address risks stemming from functional insufficiencies and incorrect environmental assumptions, which are quintessential states of uncertainty [[144](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2095809924000274), [284](https://www.engineering.org.cn/engi/EN/10.1016/j.eng.2023.10.011)]. Systematic literature reviews consistently identify the challenges of verifying these complex, learning-enabled systems as a grand challenge for the field [[65](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.14155), [67](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/386021009_Grand_Challenges_in_the_Verification_of_Autonomous_Systems)]. In robotics, failure mode analysis is a standard part of safety protocols, acknowledging that systems can fail in unexpected ways [[124](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0925231225026141)]. The practical need is clear: an AV or robot cannot afford to make a binary decision (e.g., stop or go) when its sensors provide ambiguous input about an object's identity or trajectory. A "verification pending" state would allow the system to adopt a conservative, pre-defined behavior (e.g., slow down and observe) while awaiting more conclusive data, a far safer strategy than a risky guess.

Even in the rigorously logical domain of **software engineering and formal methods**, the concept of a deferred state appears. In interactive theorem proving, LLM-powered provers propose proof steps that may be syntactically valid but semantically unclear. While the ITP will ultimately reject an invalid step, a more advanced system could first flag it for human review—a form of "pending verification" before full rejection [[285](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.12534v3), [311](https://arxiv.org/html/2601.04653v1)]. In standard software development workflows, code changes are routinely tagged as "pending verification" before they can be merged into the main codebase [[184](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.01138), [314](https://dl.acm.org/doi/pdf/10.1145/3797030)]. Bug trackers distinguish between confirmed bugs and those that are "pending verification," reflecting a similar need to hold probabilistic or uncertain results until they can be validated [[231](https://arxiv.org/html/2508.17713v1)]. This cross-domain prevalence demonstrates that the need to defer judgment is a universal feature of complex, high-stakes systems.

Based on this evidence, several future research directions are critical for advancing the viability of triadic architectures:

1.  **Develop Hybrid Benchmark Suites:** The most significant gap is the lack of empirical validation. The creation of new benchmark suites is urgently needed to test the effectiveness of triadic systems. Existing benchmarks like VeriBench, which evaluates end-to-end code verification, could be extended to include test cases that are designed to elicit a "pending" state rather than a simple pass/fail [[27](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=rWkGFmnSNl)]. Furthermore, controllable puzzle environments that allow researchers to systematically vary complexity could be used to study how models transition between probabilistic, pending, and verified states [[35](https://ml-site.cdn-apple.com/papers/the-illusion-of-thinking.pdf)].

2.  **Implement and Evaluate a Proof-of-Concept:** The most crucial next step is to build and empirically evaluate a prototype. Researchers could implement a small-scale AI system, such as a mathematical reasoner or a simple autonomous agent, with a triadic decision layer and conduct controlled experiments comparing its performance, reliability, and efficiency against a baseline binary system. This would provide concrete data on the computational overhead and, most importantly, demonstrate tangible benefits in terms of reduced costly failures and improved handling of edge cases.

3.  **Investigate Policy Learning for Triadic States:** Rather than relying on hardcoded rules for when to use the "pending" state, future work could leverage reinforcement learning to train an agent to learn optimal policies automatically. The agent could be rewarded for making correct decisions quickly and penalized for unsafe actions or unnecessary delays. This would enable the system to dynamically learn the appropriate trade-off between exploration (accepting uncertain outputs), safety (rejecting or pending), and cost, tailoring its behavior to the specific task and environment [[107](https://www.mdpi.com/2504-2289/10/1/38)].

4.  **Standardize Communication of the "Pending" State:** For triadic systems to be interoperable and scalable, the "pending" state needs a standardized representation and protocol. The frequent use of the term in technical documentation suggests a de facto need [[111](https://www.zte.com.cn/content/dam/zte-site/investorrelations/en_annual_report/d71316cf569494f3586bf63d45fdb6e8.pdf), [179](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2405.06639?)]. A formal standard, analogous to HTTP status codes, could define the different subtypes of "pending" (e.g., "awaiting human review," "awaiting sensor fusion," "semantically ambiguous") and specify how they should be communicated between different modules or systems.

In conclusion, the evidence strongly suggests that a triadic logical decision layer is a theoretically well-founded and practically motivated concept. Its core utility lies in its ability to provide a distinct, explicit state for deferred judgment, a concept already proven valuable across numerous precision-critical industries. While significant hurdles related to computational overhead and the lack of empirical validation remain, its superior expressiveness for defining clear, auditable policies on uncertainty gives it a compelling advantage over purely probabilistic methods. The path forward requires a concerted effort in empirical research to build, test, and refine these architectures, paving the way for a new generation of AI systems that are more robust, reliable, and trustworthy.
