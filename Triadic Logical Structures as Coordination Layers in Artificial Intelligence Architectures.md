# **Triadic Logical Structures as Coordination Layers in Artificial Intelligence Architectures**

## **Executive Summary**

The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence into critical infrastructure, financial markets, and healthcare has exposed a profound structural limitation in the foundational logic underlying automated decision-making. Historically, computational systems have relied upon bivalent logic, restricting the final evaluation of any process to strictly true or false, permitted or denied. While continuous probabilistic reasoning models, deep neural networks, and Bayesian inference engines allow systems to process gradients of certainty, the execution phase of these systems almost inevitably collapses into a binary threshold. This forced collapse presents a severe architectural vulnerability. It routinely forces high-ambiguity transactions into either permissive states to maintain operational liquidity and throughput, or restrictive states that completely stall system utility.  
This rigorous deep research report provides an exhaustive investigation into the potential role of triadic logical structures as a coordination layer between probabilistic reasoning systems and deterministic verification mechanisms in precision-critical artificial intelligence environments. The core research question evaluates whether a triadic decision structure could successfully manage uncertain or deferred decisions in systems that already rely heavily on probabilistic computation.  
Specifically, this analysis examines the theoretical hypothesis proposed by Lev Goukassian through his contemporary frameworks of Ternary Logic (TL) and Ternary Moral Logic (TML). The Goukassian proposal suggests that introducing a formalized third logical state, characterized conceptually as an Epistemic Hold or a Sacred Pause, can serve as a mandatory deliberative phase for artificial intelligence architectures. This third state explicitly represents decisions that are accepted for processing but are pending further verification, effectively decoupling high-speed statistical inference from deterministic governance and accountability.  
Through a comprehensive review of historical three-valued logic systems, contemporary agentic artificial intelligence architectures, cognitive security frameworks, hardware implementation constraints, and comparative uncertainty mechanisms, this report determines that triadic logical structures offer highly specific and mathematically robust architectural advantages. By explicitly separating epistemic ambiguity from immediate binary execution, triadic coordination layers prevent cascading errors in multi-agent networks and provide deterministic triggers for human-in-the-loop oversight.  
However, the successful integration of triadic logic into modern artificial intelligence requires overcoming substantial technical and physical challenges. These challenges include the strict hardware limitations of implementing multi-valued logic gates on conventional binary silicon, the latency constraints of emulation on edge devices, and the computational overhead associated with cryptographic state verification. Despite these obstacles, the theoretical alignment between historical triadic formalisms and modern decision theory suggests that shifting away from bivalent execution models may be necessary to ensure the safety, auditability, and cognitive integrity of future autonomous systems.

## **Theoretical Foundations of Triadic Logic**

The conceptualization of a third logical state is not a novel artifact of modern computer science; it possesses a rich, mathematically rigorous theoretical lineage rooted in early twentieth-century formal logic. Understanding the potential utility of triadic logic in modern artificial intelligence requires situating contemporary proposals, such as those by Goukassian, within this broader historical continuum. The transition from bivalent logic, which adheres strictly to the Principle of Excluded Middle, to many-valued logic was driven by the recognition that certain propositions intrinsically resist binary categorization.

### **The Historical Continuum of Three-Valued Systems**

The earliest recorded departure from strict bivalent logic in formal systems began with the American pragmatist and logician Charles Sanders Peirce. Around 1909, Peirce developed unpublished manuscript notes, specifically in his logic notebook identified as MS 339, detailing a many-valued logic system that recognized a lower mode of being for propositions.1 Peirce soundly rejected the idea that all propositions must be either true or false.1 He explicitly postulated three truth values: Verum for truth, Falsum for falsity, and the Limit for the third state.2  
Peirce motivated his triadic logic by examining boundary conditions and the theory of mathematical continuity. He argued that propositions predicating a property at the exact boundary of a mathematical or temporal continuity are insusceptible to strict binary categorization.3 To illustrate this, Peirce used the analogy of a black inkblot on a white sheet of paper. While any given point on the sheet is clearly either blackened or unblackened, the points located exactly on the boundary line are neither entirely black nor entirely white, because predicates referring to color require area, and a line possesses no area.3 Therefore, a proposition asserting that the boundary is black evaluates to the Limit state. In modern artificial intelligence applications, this boundary logic is highly analogous to edge cases in machine vision, boundary data in classification algorithms, and adversarial perturbations where binary forcing produces arbitrary, fragile, and mathematically unsound outputs.  
Following Peirce, formal three-valued systems were published independently by Jan Łukasiewicz in 1920 and Emil Post in 1921\.1 Łukasiewicz was motivated primarily by the philosophical and temporal problem of future contingents. He recognized that statements about future events are neither determinately true nor determinately false at the present moment, requiring a third truth value to preserve logical coherence over time.6 This conceptualization resulted in the Łukasiewicz logic, which has heavily influenced fuzzy logic and continuous reasoning systems.2  
Stephen Kleene later introduced his own three-valued logics, which were explicitly motivated by the realities of computational procedures.7 In Kleene's strong logic, a procedural function may yield a True or False output, but it may also fail to terminate. When a procedure fails to terminate or resolve, the corresponding truth value is undefined or unknown, represented by a third state.7 Kleene distinguished critically between serial and parallel evaluation of these states. In a parallel disjunction operation, if one independent procedure returns a True value, the overall system can confidently return True even if the second procedure remains in the undefined state.7 This foundational concept maps directly to modern asynchronous artificial intelligence operations and distributed computing, where partial data streams must be evaluated independently without stalling the entire computational pipeline.

The interpretation of the third state fundamentally alters the mathematical mechanics of the logical operators utilized within the system. Peirce defined highly specific binary and unary operations in his matrix tables, notably the operators for conjunction and disjunction.2 These operators fail standard distribution laws, indicating that Peirce was developing systems that were either paracomplete or paraconsistent.2

### **Ternary Logic and Ternary Moral Logic in Contemporary Systems**

Lev Goukassian's proposed frameworks, Ternary Logic and Ternary Moral Logic, represent a contemporary applied evolution of these classical mathematical systems.11 While historical models viewed the third state primarily as a descriptive semantic category for unknown, undefined, or continuous boundary conditions, Goukassian reframes the third state as an active, mandatory architectural mechanism for economic and artificial intelligence governance.12  
In the Goukassian frameworks, the logical structure utilizes three distinct operational values: Proceed, Halt, and the Epistemic Hold.12 The Proceed state permits immediate execution when signals are clear, context is verified, and uncertainty is tightly bounded. The Halt state rejects execution when harm, mathematical impossibility, or direct policy violations are evident. The critical architectural innovation lies in the intermediate state, known as the Epistemic Hold or the Sacred Zero.12  
Unlike Kleene's undefined state, which passively observes that a computation has not yet terminated, or Peirce's Limit state, which statically describes a boundary condition, the Epistemic Hold is a prescriptive operational trigger.7 It actively enforces a deterministic deliberative pause within the computational system.12 The Ternary Logic framework asserts that when internal uncertainty exceeds a defined statistical threshold, or when moral and logical rules directly conflict within the processing graph, the system is architecturally forbidden from collapsing the decision into a forced binary guess.16  
Instead, the artificial intelligence system must hold its state, generate an immutable cryptographic log detailing the parameters of the uncertainty, and escalate the decision to an external oracle, a secondary heuristic layer, or a human steward.15 Therefore, Ternary Logic and Ternary Moral Logic introduce a substantial operational extension relative to historical models. They transition the third logical state from a descriptive condition of epistemology into an actionable, legally auditable mechanism for accountability, transparency, and systemic safety.12

## **Conceptual Role of a Third Logical State**

Introducing a third logical state into a complex artificial intelligence architecture fundamentally redefines the epistemology of the autonomous system. In standard bivalent architectures, uncertainty is invariably handled upstream of the final decision gate. Probabilistic networks calculate a continuous floating-point value, such as a confidence score of 0.51, which is then subjected to a hard-coded threshold function. If the threshold parameter is 0.50, the system blindly rounds up and proceeds with the binary execution of True. The operational reality of the underlying uncertainty is completely erased at the point of action, leaving no trace of the system's internal doubt in the execution log.  
The conceptual role of triadic states is to preserve the mathematical reality of uncertainty across the execution boundary.17 The third state explicitly maps to conditions of deferred evaluation, incomplete verification, and high-stakes ambiguity.17 This capability is critical for environments where artificial intelligence must navigate complex real-world variables without collapsing nuance into arbitrary binary actions.

### **Ambiguity and Deferred Evaluation Pipelines**

In software engineering and data pipeline terminology, the triadic state closely mirrors the operational concept of a package being accepted but pending.18 When a query, data package, or sensor input enters an artificial intelligence system, a bivalent framework forces an immediate resolution. The input is either accepted and processed, or rejected and discarded. In a triadic framework, the system can acknowledge receipt of the data, accept it into the operational queue, but explicitly flag the execution status as pending due to inadequate certainty or missing context.18  
This pending condition is not a failure state or a crash; it is a highly functional, deliberate queuing state. The pending phase becomes the designated domain where asynchronous verification occurs, whether through secondary logic gates, human review, or cross-referencing external data lakes.18 In project management workflows and software deployment systems, this accepted-pending state ensures that tasks are neither lost in transit nor prematurely executed before automated security checks conclude.18 Applying this concept to artificial intelligence allows a model to acknowledge a prompt, construct a partial logical framework, but pause execution until specific missing parameters are supplied by the user or an external database.

### **The Sacred Pause and Active Hesitation**

This deferred state is particularly vital for precision-critical systems where the cost of a false positive or a false negative is asymmetric and potentially catastrophic. Goukassian philosophically and technically identifies this condition as the core of the Sacred Pause, an embedded ethical and computational mandate requiring the machine to hesitate when truth is uncertain.12  
Conceptually, this active hesitation prevents automated systems from prioritizing continuous throughput and execution speed over accuracy and safety. It provides the temporal space necessary for a genuine human-machine partnership, shifting the machine from an autonomous, unquestionable arbiter into an analytical collaborator that actively signals its own epistemic limits.19 By encoding hesitation as a valid computational output, triadic logic systems formally recognize that in many high-stakes economic, medical, and strategic scenarios, doing nothing and requesting more information is statistically and morally superior to making a low-confidence guess.17 This represents a paradigm shift from viewing uncertainty as an error to be minimized, to viewing uncertainty as a signal to be managed.

## **Architectural Possibilities for Triadic Decision Structures**

To operationalize the conceptual benefits of a triadic system, artificial intelligence architectures must integrate the third state at specific, high-leverage junctures within their topologies. The precise placement of a triadic decision layer determines how a system balances inference speed with verification thoroughness, and how it handles recursive logic loops.

### **Coordination Layers in Multi-Agent Networks**

Modern artificial intelligence development is rapidly transitioning from isolated, monolithic request-response architectures to dynamic multi-agent networks.20 In these sophisticated environments, distinct specialized agents dynamically discover capabilities, share context, and execute complex, multi-step workflows across permeable system boundaries.20 However, empirical testing reveals a critical, often fatal vulnerability in unstructured multi-agent architectures.  
When autonomous agents operate in a continuous sequence without structured coordination logic, their individual errors do not cancel out; they cascade and compound.21 Rigorous research conducted by Google DeepMind teams evaluated 180 network configurations across various agent architectures and large language model families.21 The findings demonstrated that unstructured multi-agent networks, frequently described as a bag of agents, amplify output errors by up to 17.2 times compared to single-agent baselines.21 Because the output of one agent serves directly as the input for the next, a hallucination or low-confidence assumption at step one becomes a verified fact by step three.  
A triadic logical structure provides an ideal, mathematically sound coordination layer to arrest this severe error cascade. By embedding triadic evaluation gates between distinct agents, the architecture fundamentally prevents an agent from passing a low-confidence hallucination downstream. Instead of forcing a bivalent True or False output when generating intermediate data, an agent encountering ambiguous information outputs the Epistemic Hold state.22 The central network coordination layer interprets this specific state not as a system failure, but as an explicit architectural signal to route the query to a specialized verification agent, invoke an external search tool, or trigger a complete abstention mechanism.20 This structured topology creates firewalls against error amplification.

### **The Dual-Lane Governance Architecture**

A significant architectural innovation proposed to support triadic logic without sacrificing computational performance is the Dual-Lane Architecture.13 A major historical criticism of incorporating deep verification, human oversight, and cryptographic logging into automated systems is the resulting computational bottleneck.24 If every single algorithmic decision must be exhaustively verified and logged to a blockchain or immutable ledger, the system cannot possibly operate at the sub-millisecond latencies required for algorithmic financial trading, autonomous vehicle navigation, or real-time network defense.  
The Dual-Lane Architecture resolves this critical friction by cleanly decoupling the high-speed inference layer from the rigorous governance layer.13 The architecture operates through two parallel tracks:

1. The Inference Lane operates at extreme velocity, targeting user-visible latencies of two milliseconds or less.13 It utilizes highly optimized probabilistic models to evaluate incoming data streams. If the statistical confidence exceeds the strict upper threshold, it immediately outputs the Proceed command. If it falls below the lower threshold, it immediately outputs the Halt command.  
2. The Governance and Verification Lane handles the exceptions. If the high-speed inference lane encounters data that falls within the established ambiguity threshold, it triggers the Epistemic Hold state. The decision package is immediately routed into the slower, asynchronous verification lane.13 Here, the system executes computationally heavy operations: querying external data oracles, generating cryptographic evidence chains using Merkle batching, appending SHA-256 hashes to distributed ledgers, and awaiting manual steward resolution.15

This parallel architecture ensures that the system's baseline performance never permanently outpaces its accountability.23 The vast majority of standard, high-confidence transactions process instantly via the bivalent extremes, while the triadic midpoint acts as an intelligent, selective pressure valve strictly for complex, dangerous, or legally ambiguous edge cases.

### **Verification Pipelines and Logic-Optimization Trees**

Triadic structures can also be positioned within formal verification pipelines and hierarchical logic trees. In enterprise and institutional settings, automated work is increasingly organized as a strict delegation pipeline. Within this pipeline, a specific task may be performed manually by a human worker, delegated entirely to an artificial intelligence agent, or delegated to an artificial intelligence agent subject to strict deterministic verification.26  
Within this specific pipeline architecture, a triadic decision gate evaluates the probabilistic output of a classifier or generative model. The gate applies symbolic logic, regulatory constraints, or algorithmic rules to the output. If the output conforms perfectly to expected parameters, it is automatically accepted. If it violates safety parameters, it is automatically rejected. However, if the output is logically sound but lacks sufficient external evidence or historical precedent, the gate assigns the verification pending state. The output is then routed to a secondary logic-optimization behavior tree for deeper analysis.27 In autonomous underwater vehicles and drone swarms, this groundbreaking triadic logic structure uniquely integrates state judgment and action elements, dramatically reducing the reliance on manual human design for cooperative decision rules while maintaining rigorous safety boundaries in dynamic environments.27

## **Comparison With Existing Uncertainty Handling Mechanisms**

To rigorously evaluate the architectural utility of triadic logic, it must be compared directly against the existing methodologies utilized in machine learning for managing uncertainty. The dominant paradigms in contemporary computer science rely heavily on probabilistic reasoning and continuous mathematical distributions.

### **Probabilistic Confidence Scores and Bayesian Inference**

The primary and most widespread alternative to triadic logic is Bayesian inference, which maintains uncertainty as a continuous probability distribution across a complex network of variables.35 Bayesian models calculate expected loss values and assign specific mathematical utilities to different outcomes, aiming to minimize the overall risk of incorrect classification over a large dataset.36 In this context, confidence scores represent the precise mathematical likelihood of a specific state being true.  
The core architectural weakness of continuous probability in operational systems is that execution environments in the physical world are inherently discrete. An autonomous vehicle must either apply the brakes or accelerate; a high-frequency trade must either execute on the exchange or cancel. Therefore, all continuous probabilities must eventually be resolved through thresholding functions. When systems utilize simple thresholding to convert a probability into an action, they recreate the exact vulnerabilities of bivalent logic.  
Triadic logic, particularly implemented through Three-Way Decision theory, addresses this fundamental flaw by explicitly formalizing the threshold gap.36 Instead of utilizing a single binary boundary, Three-Way Decision theory defines two distinct boundaries: an upper threshold and a lower threshold. If the calculated probability exceeds the upper threshold, the system accepts the proposition. If the probability falls below the lower threshold, the system rejects it. Crucially, if the probability falls between the lower and upper thresholds, the system automatically defaults to the third state of formalized uncertainty.29 Therefore, triadic logic does not replace Bayesian reasoning; rather, it acts as a superior, formalized coordination layer for executing Bayesian outputs in discrete environments.

### **Conformal Prediction and Explicit Uncertainty Models**

Large Language Models notoriously struggle with probability calibration and self-reflection. A generative model might output a highly inaccurate hallucination while simultaneously assigning it a very high internal probability score.37 Recent empirical studies analyzing truthfulness in Large Language Models demonstrate that extracting raw probabilities is often completely insufficient for measuring actual veracity.38  
Conformal prediction provides a mathematical methodology for generating guaranteed prediction regions, but it requires a robust architectural mechanism to handle the resulting ambiguous outcomes.37 Researchers increasingly advocate for a strict three-valued logic approach to explicitly model veracity in Large Language Models, forcing the architecture to distinguish clearly between true statements, false statements, and statements that are neither.38 Without an explicit third state embedded in the logic layer, models forced to classify highly ambiguous statements exhibit highly erratic behavior and rely on spurious linguistic proxies rather than genuine veracity signals.38 By implementing a triadic architecture, artificial intelligence developers can explicitly program abstention mechanisms, allowing models to confidently output "I do not know" rather than fabricating data.

### **The Belief Box Technique in Symbolic AI**

In advanced symbolic artificial intelligence and complex agentic architectures, uncertainty is frequently managed using dynamic epistemic systems, most notably the Belief Box technique.39 A Belief Box embeds specific propositions alongside their current confidence scores directly into the working memory of an agent.39 This replicates a symbolic memory structure that can be continuously referenced, debated, and updated during agent interaction.  
This technique relies heavily on the foundational principles of three-valued logic as a boundary case. It allows the agent to temporarily hold a proposition as indeterminate or partially supported until external verification provides sufficient evidentiary weight to push the proposition definitively into true or false territory.39

## **Technical Challenges and Counterarguments**

While the theoretical elegance and architectural safety benefits of triadic logic are substantial, operationalizing these complex systems introduces highly specific technical complications and legitimate engineering counterarguments. The transition from theory to deployment requires addressing severe hardware constraints and computational bottlenecks.

### **Hardware Feasibility and Implementation Friction**

Modern computer hardware is entirely optimized from the silicon level upward for binary execution. The silicon foundries, memory registers, and arithmetic logic units of contemporary central processing units and graphics processing units operate exclusively on two states driven by strict voltage thresholds. Implementing pure ternary logic at the bare-metal hardware level requires the fabrication of Multi-Valued Logic circuits.40  
Academic and industrial research into hardware-level ternary circuits focuses heavily on Carbon Nanotube Field-Effect Transistors, tunneling-based complementary metal-oxide-semiconductors, and depletion-mode MOSFETs.41 While these experimental physical components demonstrate marked improvements in power-delay products and require significantly fewer interconnects than their binary equivalents, they face severe technical challenges regarding scalable mass fabrication, thermal stability, and high production costs.40 As a result, the fabrication of a commercially practical Carbon Nanotube Field-Effect Transistor for mass-market artificial intelligence hardware remains questionable.42  
Without native ternary hardware processors, triadic logic must be emulated at the software or middleware layer on top of standard binary hardware. On Edge Field-Programmable Gate Arrays, implementing ternary matrix multiplications requires deploying numerous look-up table-based multiplexers. This approach results in highly repetitive computational patterns and highly inefficient interactions with on-chip and off-chip memory.44 Because large language models are fundamentally memory-bound, this software emulation introduces severe processing latency, mitigating many of the computational efficiency gains expected from triadic coordination.44

### **Computational Overhead of Governance Architectures**

Frameworks like Ternary Moral Logic and the Dual-Lane Architecture demand that the third state triggers extensive cryptographic logging and deterministic verification.12 Implementing multi-chain anchoring, SHA-256 hashing, and Merkle tree batching to create legally court-admissible evidence logs requires substantial computational overhead.25  
While Goukassian's highly optimized specifications target overheads of less than two milliseconds for user-visible actions and less than 500 milliseconds for full block anchoring, scaling this cryptographic infrastructure across highly parallelized, high-frequency environments poses a massive data ingestion challenge.25 As the frequency of model outputs increases in environments like autonomous driving or massive Internet of Things networks, the communication overhead required to maintain these immutable ledgers could easily bottleneck centralized server infrastructures.46 Furthermore, the architectural complexity of managing dual systems, where inference and governance operate in parallel but must synchronize perfectly upon encountering an Epistemic Hold, dramatically increases the development burden, testing requirements, and maintenance costs for software engineering teams.24

### **The Innovation Velocity Counterargument**

From a broader strategic and economic perspective, imposing strict evidentiary constraints and mandatory Epistemic Holds on artificial intelligence could severely hinder innovation velocity.24 Critics logically argue that existing probabilistic systems, combined with standard safety rails, already capture uncertainty effectively enough for the vast majority of commercial purposes.24  
If an enterprise integrates Ternary Moral Logic, their artificial intelligence systems will intentionally pause and request human intervention during highly ambiguous scenarios. Meanwhile, a competitor utilizing standard bivalent thresholds will continue processing transactions and generating outputs without interruption. Even if the bivalent system occasionally hallucinates or produces a costly error, the overall throughput, data accumulation, and speed to market may economically outperform the slower, highly verified triadic system.24 Trading inference speed and frictionless operation for deep learning safety and cryptographic auditability remains a highly contentious proposition in hyper-competitive technological markets where market share is often dictated by deployment velocity.

## **Future Research Directions**

If triadic logical structures are to transcend theoretical proposals and become a realistic, scalable architectural standard for artificial intelligence, academic and industrial research must focus intensively on alleviating the existing hardware friction points and expanding the framework's interoperability.  
First, hardware-software co-design must become a primary research vector. Future engineering efforts should heavily investigate the integration of tunneling-based complementary metal-oxide-semiconductors or depletion-mode MOSFET technologies directly alongside existing binary memory architectures.42 Developing hybrid processing units that can natively compute the three-state logic required for the Epistemic Hold without software emulation will dramatically reduce latency overhead and power consumption.  
Second, research must advance the field of dynamic threshold optimization. The mathematical boundaries defining the entry into the third state currently require careful, static manual tuning by data scientists.36 Research into self-evolving architectures and meta-learning algorithms that can dynamically adjust these thresholds in real-time based on environmental risk assessment, context shifts, and adversarial threat levels will make triadic systems vastly more adaptable and resilient.47  
Third, the standardization of verification oracles is critical. When a system enters an Epistemic Hold, it relies entirely on external data oracles, consensus mechanisms, or human stewards for resolution.15 Establishing standardized, cryptographically secure, and ultra-low-latency application programming interfaces for these decentralized verification oracles is essential to ensuring the hold state functions as a brief pause rather than a permanent system deadlock.  
Finally, exploring how triadic logic interacts with quantum computing environments and advanced symbolic mathematics, such as the quantum-lattice anomaly mapping proposed in the Spectral-Fractal-Symbolic Intelligence framework, could yield profound breakthroughs in ultra-secure, unhackable defense architectures for national security applications.33

## **Conclusion**

This rigorous research report investigated the core technical question: could a triadic logical structure function as a coordination layer that manages uncertain or deferred decisions in AI systems that already rely on probabilistic reasoning and deterministic verification?  
The extensive analysis of theoretical logic, contemporary architectural models, and empirical data strongly indicates that triadic logical structures provide a highly meaningful and structurally necessary coordination mechanism for advanced artificial intelligence architectures. While probabilistic and Bayesian systems excel at mapping continuous gradients of uncertainty, bivalent execution architectures fundamentally fail to preserve that critical uncertainty at the exact point of action. By explicitly formalizing a third state, whether defined theoretically as the limit by Peirce, computationally as undefined by Kleene, or architecturally as the Epistemic Hold by Goukassian, autonomous systems gain a deterministic, mathematically sound mechanism for managing profound ambiguity.  
The introduction of this third logical state allows artificial intelligence pipelines to safely defer decisions, prevent the catastrophic amplification of errors across complex multi-agent networks, and provide the essential temporal space required for cryptographic logging and human oversight. The Dual-Lane Architecture proves that such systems can effectively decouple raw execution velocity from rigorous verification, offering a highly practical implementation pathway for high-stakes environments including financial enforcement, autonomous navigation, and medical diagnostics.  
However, the transition to triadic coordination layers is neither trivial nor frictionless. It requires overcoming significant hardware optimization challenges, managing the intense computational overhead of immutable ledgers, and navigating the difficult economic trade-offs associated with deliberate system hesitation. Despite these hurdles, as artificial intelligence systems scale exponentially in power, speed, and autonomy, the structural inability of bivalent logic to safely manage the unknown will become increasingly dangerous and legally untenable. Triadic logic provides the necessary architectural maturity, ensuring that when intelligent machines inevitably encounter the limits of their statistical certainty, they possess the formal, encoded capacity to pause, verify, and proceed with demonstrable integrity.
